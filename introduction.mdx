Velobase is an open-source, high-performance billing ledger built for AI applications. This page explains the problem it solves, the core concepts you'll work with, and how to choose the right billing flow for your use case.

## The problem with billing AI apps

Billing for AI workloads is hard because you often don't know the exact cost before the work starts. A streaming LLM call might consume 200 tokens or 2,000 — you can't know upfront. Standard payment flows assume a known price at charge time, which breaks down for inference, async tasks, and multi-step pipelines.

Velobase solves this by acting as a high-speed credit bank inside your application. You deposit credits to a customer's balance, and your app deducts them as AI work happens — safely, without race conditions or negative balances.

<Note>
  Velobase is API-first. Every operation — depositing credits, charging customers, checking balances — is a REST call. There is no SDK required to get started.
</Note>

## Core concepts

### Credits

Credits are the unit of value in Velobase. You define what one credit is worth in your application — it could map to a dollar amount, a token budget, or an abstract points system. Customers hold a balance of credits, and your app deducts from that balance as they use AI features.

### Customers

A customer in Velobase maps to a user or account in your system. You identify customers by a `customer_id` that you provide — typically your own user ID or account ID. Velobase creates the customer automatically on first deposit, so you don't need a separate registration step.

### Balance breakdown

Every customer balance has four components:

| Field       | Description                                                         |
| ----------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `total`     | All credits ever deposited for this customer                        |
| `used`      | Credits that have been permanently consumed                         |
| `frozen`    | Credits reserved for an in-progress operation, not yet settled      |
| `available` | Credits available to spend (`total − used − frozen`)                |

<Tip>
  Always check `available` — not `total` — when deciding whether a customer can afford an operation. Frozen credits are already committed to another in-flight request.
</Tip>

### Direct deduction

Direct deduction charges a customer immediately for a known amount. Call `POST /v1/billing/deduct` with the cost and Velobase deducts it from the customer's available balance in one step. Use this when the price is fixed before the work starts.

### Freeze-consume model

The freeze-consume flow is a two-step charge for operations where the final cost is only known after execution:

1. **Freeze** — before the work starts, reserve an estimated (maximum) amount from the customer's balance. The frozen credits are no longer available to spend elsewhere.
2. **Consume** — after the work completes, settle the actual cost. Velobase deducts the real amount and automatically returns any unused frozen credits.

If the operation is cancelled before it completes, call `unfreeze` instead of `consume` to release the reserved credits.

## When to use each billing flow

| Scenario                                     | Recommended flow     |
| -------------------------------------------- | -------------------- |
| Image generation with a fixed credit price   | Direct deduction     |
| Known API call cost (e.g., a fixed fee)      | Direct deduction     |
| LLM inference (token count unknown upfront)  | Freeze-consume       |
| Async AI task with variable compute time     | Freeze-consume       |
| Multi-step pipeline with uncertain total     | Freeze-consume       |

<Note>
  When in doubt, prefer the freeze-consume flow. It protects against negative balances even if your estimated cost is wrong, and it makes your ledger auditable at every step.
</Note>

## High concurrency by design

Velobase is built to handle thousands of simultaneous credit operations without race conditions. Balance checks and deductions are atomic, so two concurrent requests for the same customer cannot both succeed if only one has enough credits.
